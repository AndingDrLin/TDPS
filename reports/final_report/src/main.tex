\begin{abstract}
This paper presents the design, implementation, and evaluation of an autonomous line-following robot that integrates radar obstacle avoidance and LoRa checkpoint telemetry on a single STM32F407VET6 microcontroller without an RTOS. The control architecture uses a simplified 6-parameter PD plus curvature feedforward (PD+kff) controller, derived through root-cause failure analysis of a legacy 62-parameter PID system whose competing mechanisms prevented convergent tuning. Parameters were optimized through two-round systematic sweeping (63 groups) on an offline simulator that compiles the identical C control code against a physics harness, achieving a 30--40$\times$ speedup over on-car testing. Under the optimal configuration the controller achieves a simulator regression score of 97.65/100 with 100\% line detection rate and zero off-line time; the full-course simulation achieves 92.81/100 with 99.6\% detection; the feedforward term reduces steering correction standard deviation by 19\% versus pure PD control. A non-blocking tick architecture preserves the 10\,ms control loop during concurrent radar parsing and LoRa transmission, while weak-symbol extension hooks decouple communication and radar subsystems from the core line-following state machine. On-car validation confirms stable tracking on straight segments, curves, and U-turns. The simulator-first methodology, parameter decoupling from 62 to 6 dimensions, and the dual role of the 22\,cm sensor preview distance (simultaneously a damping advantage and a junction-handling challenge) are analyzed as design trade-offs that extend beyond this platform.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} University of Glasgow Team Design Project (TDPS) requires each team to design a robot capable of completing three mandatory tasks --- line-following, radar obstacle avoidance, and LoRa checkpoint telemetry --- on a single microcontroller without an operating system. Meeting this requirement on an STM32F407VET6 (Cortex-M4, 168\,MHz)~\cite{cortex_m4_trm} demands not only individual subsystem competence but also their coexistence within a deterministic 10\,ms control loop, making integration, not control design alone, the primary challenge~\cite{siegwart2011autonomous, astrom1995pid}.

\subsection{Problem Statement and Scope}

Three tightly coupled constraints govern the design. First, the 22\,cm sensor preview --- the distance between the sensor array and the drive axle --- simultaneously provides natural derivative damping and creates a geometric mismatch at junctions and U-turns that no amount of gain tuning can resolve. Second, the HLK-LD2410S 24\,GHz radar offers only forward distance information without lateral discrimination, so obstacle avoidance must be executed as timed open-loop maneuvers based on heuristic direction selection. Third, LoRa AT commands block the CPU for 50--200\,ms per transaction, requiring architectural isolation so that communication never disrupts the 10\,ms line-following control period. The core engineering tension is therefore between control-loop timing, sensor geometry, and communication latency --- three constraints that must be satisfied simultaneously, not sequentially.

The scope is explicitly bounded: this project addresses a single differential-drive robot with a fixed sensor configuration. Multi-robot coordination, visual SLAM, and adaptive road-surface detection are outside scope.

\subsection{Literature Survey}

Line-following on resource-constrained microcontrollers has been explored through several control paradigms. Classical PID control~\cite{astrom1995pid, ziegler1942optimum} remains prevalent due to its minimal computational requirements~\cite{braunl2006embedded}, yet established tuning methods such as Ziegler--Nichols and SIMC~\cite{skogestad2003simple} assume sensor--actuator co-location --- an assumption violated when the sensor is physically separated from the drive wheels. Fuzzy logic controllers~\cite{lee1990fuzzy} relax parameter sensitivity but sacrifice analytical transparency, hindering systematic failure diagnosis. Model predictive control~\cite{kuhne2004mpc} delivers high tracking performance at the cost of accurate dynamic models and floating-point computation budgets unavailable on a Cortex-M4 running bare-metal firmware.

Integrating sensing and communication on a shared microcontroller introduces a distinct set of constraints. Single-chip 24\,GHz mmWave radar modules~\cite{ld2410s_datasheet} provide compact range sensing but are inherently forward-only, precluding closed-loop lateral correction during obstacle circumvention. LoRa communication~\cite{lorawan_spec, ewm22a_datasheet} offers long-range, low-power data links at the expense of low data rates and AT-command blocking latencies that conflict with hard real-time control. Existing literature addresses these components individually but does not resolve simultaneous integration under the constraint of a 10\,ms control loop on a single microcontroller without an RTOS. This work simplifies and co-optimizes the control, sensing, and communication subsystems within these coupled constraints.

\section{Overall System Design Approach}

\subsection{System Architecture Overview}

The system adopts a layered architecture of Perception--Control--Actuation--Communication, orchestrated by a single-threaded main loop with peripheral interrupts. A tick-based non-blocking design was chosen over both cooperative scheduling and a preemptive RTOS. The task set comprises three bounded periodic sources --- a 10\,ms line-sensor sampling tick, a radar UART parser at approximately 20\,Hz, and discrete LoRa checkpoint events --- each with measured worst-case execution time well below the main-loop period. A cooperative scheduler would add explicit yield points without improving temporal isolation for this small task set, and an RTOS would introduce scheduling jitter and stack overhead; a bare-metal super-loop is the simplest deterministic solution. \subsection{Hardware Platform and Constraints}

The robot employs a differential-drive chassis with a 16\,cm wheel track. An 8-channel Yahboom grayscale sensor array communicates via UART at 9600\,baud and is mounted 22\,cm ahead of the drive axle center --- a geometry that directly governs the relationship between sensor measurements and vehicle position. An HLK-LD2410S 24\,GHz radar module and an EWM22A-900BWL22S LoRa module are integrated via separate UART interfaces. The STM32F407VET6 (Cortex-M4, 168\,MHz, 512\,KB Flash, 192\,KB SRAM) serves as the central controller \cite{stm32f407_ref}. Table~\ref{tab:hw-specs} summarizes the key hardware components.

The power subsystem uses a two-stage regulation chain. A LM2596S buck converter steps the battery voltage down to 5\,V at up to 3\,A to supply the DRV8874 motor drivers. An AMS1117-3.3 LDO then provides a low-ripple 3.3\,V rail for the MCU, LoRa, and radar logic, isolating sensitive digital loads from the switching noise of the motor stage. During the PCB V1 to V2 revision, an LM74700 reverse-polarity protection controller and an NTMFS5C628NLT1G MOSFET were added at the battery input to guard against reverse insertion and surge stress before the buck stage.

Two geometric constraints govern the control design. The 22\,cm sensor preview means that detected line features lead the drive wheels by approximately $22/v$~seconds at speed $v$, providing natural preview damping via the derivative term but requiring explicit lead compensation at sharp junctions. The 16\,cm wheel track constrains the minimum differential speed required for a given turning radius.

\subsection{Project Planning and Workflow}

Development was organized into six milestones: M1 (chassis and peripheral bring-up), M2 (basic line-following closed-loop), M3 (line-loss recovery), M4 (LoRa checkpoint integration), M5 (radar decision integration), and M6 (full-system integration).

A major architecture refactoring was performed during the final integration phase. The original 62-parameter PID controller was replaced by a 6-parameter PD+curvature-feedforward controller. Root-cause analysis during the tuning phase identified seven mutually competing mechanisms in the legacy system --- deadband, soft-zone shaping, integral accumulation, speed-scaled gain, output rate limiting, derivative double-filtering, and a six-level speed priority chain --- each of which interacted non-transitively with the others, rendering systematic parameter optimization infeasible. The offline simulation harness (Section~\ref{subsec:sim}) enabled this refactoring by allowing each legacy mechanism to be individually disabled and its performance delta measured under reproducible conditions. \subsection{Offline Simulation and Regression Strategy}
\label{subsec:sim}

The offline simulation harness compiles the identical C control code (via GCC for PC, not cross-compiled for STM32) against a modeled environment. Each test scenario is defined in a CSV configuration specifying track geometry (circle, figure-8, patio, or full-competition map), initial vehicle pose, and fault-injection parameters. The vehicle dynamics use a first-order inertial model with the physical 16\,cm wheel track for trajectory integration. The virtual 8-channel sensor computes per-channel readings from ground-truth position and injects configurable Gaussian noise, multiplicative gain error, additive bias, and dropout events.

Rather than a single pass/fail threshold, the harness enforces progressive quality gates. The quick gate ($\sim$30\,s) runs all 16 baseline scenarios requiring overall score $\geq$82, detection rate $\geq$94\%, and max off-line time $\leq$0.35\,s. The stability gate ($\sim$5\,min) repeats scenarios with 10 random seeds, requiring worst-case score $\geq$80 across seeds. The full-course gate ($\sim$2\,min) tests the complete competition map at competition speed with sequential checkpoint triggering and zero-collision requirements.

Measured against the prior on-car cycle of 15--20 minutes per parameter data point (re-flash, place on track, observe, record), the simulator evaluates each configuration in approximately 30 seconds --- a measured 30--40$\times$ throughput improvement that enabled the complete 63-group parameter sweep within a single session.

\section{Subsystem Design and Solutions}

\subsection{Line-Following Subsystem}

\subsubsection{Sensor Processing Pipeline}

The 8-channel Yahboom sensor communicates via UART, outputting 8 bytes per frame representing per-channel grayscale intensity. The processing pipeline converts raw sensor readings into a robust position estimate by applying three processing stages that address the dominant noise sources on physical tracks: electrical noise (thermal and quantization), optical artifacts (floor dust, map creases, reflective glare), and transient dropouts from surface irregularities.

The first stage is \emph{ambient-light invariance}: per-channel readings are normalized against min/max calibration values collected during an initial swing-sweep phase, mapping raw values to a consistent range so that the position estimate is independent of ambient illumination, battery voltage, and sensor aging. The second stage is \emph{noise rejection}: a first-order IIR low-pass filter attenuates high-frequency disturbances without introducing significant phase lag at the control bandwidth. The third stage is \emph{sub-sensor resolution}: rather than thresholding each channel to binary on/off, a weighted centroid $e = \sum w_i \cdot \text{norm}_i / \sum \text{norm}_i$ with linearly distributed weights computes a continuous position signal whose effective resolution far exceeds the 8 discrete sensor elements, producing an error signal suitable for derivative-based feedforward control.

In addition to the position error $e$, the pipeline extracts auxiliary quality signals that support downstream noise rejection, loss recovery, and interference suppression. Line presence is determined by a multi-condition AND of signal sum, peak value, and contrast each exceeding their respective thresholds, preventing false detection from single-channel activation caused by floor artifacts. Fig.~\ref{fig:sensor} summarizes the complete pipeline.

\subsubsection{Simplified PD + Curvature Feedforward Controller}

The controller employs a simplified 6-parameter architecture. The steering correction is computed as:
%
\begin{equation}
    \text{correction} = k_p \cdot e + k_d \cdot \dot{e} + k_{\text{ff}} \cdot \dot{e} \cdot v
    \label{eq:correction}
\end{equation}
%
where $e$ is the weighted position error, $\dot{e}$ is its discrete-time derivative, and $v$ is the smoothed vehicle speed. The vehicle speed follows a continuous function of position error:
%
\begin{equation}
    v = v_{\text{base}} - (v_{\text{base}} - v_{\text{min}}) \cdot \frac{|e|}{1750}
    \label{eq:speed}
\end{equation}
%
with IIR smoothing ($\alpha=0.4$). Differential commands $v_L = v + \text{correction}$, $v_R = v - \text{correction}$ are both clamped by $C_{\text{max}}=300$. Fig.~\ref{fig:control} presents the control block diagram.

\begin{figure}[!t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig5_control_block.pdf}
    \caption{PD+curvature feedforward control block diagram. Three parallel branches (P: proportional, D: derivative, FF: feedforward) combine to produce the steering correction. The speed branch computes vehicle speed as a continuous function of $|e|$. Optimal parameter values are annotated on each branch.}
    \label{fig:control}
\end{figure}

The 62-parameter system was reduced to 6 through root-cause failure analysis. The seven legacy mechanisms were classified as threshold-based (deadband, soft zone), gain-competing (integral, speed-scaled gain, derivative double-filtering), or rate-limiting (output rate limiter, speed priority chain), then individually disabled under controlled conditions with documented performance deltas (Table~\ref{tab:elimination}).

The derivative term $k_d \cdot \dot{e}$ serves a dual role. Beyond classical damping, the 22\,cm sensor preview means $\dot{e}$ encodes upcoming curvature information --- the sensor sees line curvature approximately $22/v$ seconds before the drive wheels reach that point, so the derivative term acts as both a damper and a preview gain. The curvature feedforward term $k_{\text{ff}} \cdot \dot{e} \cdot v$ provides anticipatory steering before $e$ grows large; the optimal $k_{\text{ff}}$ reduced steering correction standard deviation by 19\% compared to pure PD, while excessive feedforward gain introduced straight-line micro-oscillation through noise amplification.

Parameters were determined through two rounds of systematic simulator-based sweeping: a 36-group coarse sweep establishing the optimal region, and a 27-group fine sweep determining the optimal $k_{\text{ff}}$. Table~\ref{tab:core-params} lists the six parameters, their optimal values, functions, and optimization methods.

\subsubsection{State Machine and Edge-Case Handling}

The line-following state machine progresses through WAIT\_START $\rightarrow$ CALIBRATING $\rightarrow$ RUNNING, with RUNNING continuously monitoring edge conditions through dedicated parameter groups tuned after --- not before --- the core PD+kff parameters were stabilized. This ordering is critical: edge-case handlers must be tuned against a stable control baseline, otherwise their parameters absorb control errors and become fragile when the baseline changes. Within RUNNING, action selection follows an explicit priority hierarchy: line-loss recovery (highest), fork handling, obstacle avoidance, lead compensation, then PID control (lowest). This explicit arbitration makes per-cycle decisions predictable and debuggable.

When the sensor detects a special line type (T-junction, right-angle turn, or U-turn apex), the drive axle center is approximately 22\,cm behind the detection point. Because the sensor sees line features that far ahead of the wheels, controller retuning alone cannot handle the geometric mismatch --- the controller would need to simultaneously account for normal curvature tracking and the extreme entry geometry of junctions. Instead, the lead compensation sequence explicitly advances the vehicle straight at reduced speed until the axle reaches the junction, providing geometric compensation rather than attempting to tune a single controller for all line features. Fig.~\ref{fig:state_machine} shows the complete state transition diagram, and Table~\ref{tab:transitions} lists the transition conditions.

\subsection{Radar Obstacle Avoidance Subsystem}

\textit{Sensor characteristics and limitations.} The HLK-LD2410S is a 24\,GHz frequency-modulated continuous-wave (FMCW) radar module~\cite{ld2410s_datasheet} communicating via UART at 115200\,baud. Its single-antenna design provides range measurements along the boresight axis only; it offers no lateral discrimination, angular resolution, or multi-target separation capability. This forward-only sensing envelope is the fundamental constraint governing the entire avoidance design: without knowledge of obstacle width or lateral position, closed-loop trajectory correction during circumvention is infeasible.

\textit{Parsing approach.} A non-blocking tick function parses the serial buffer incrementally, consuming available bytes without waiting for a complete frame. A pure state-machine parser supports three packet formats: custom test frames, LD2410S minimal 5-byte frames, and standard 80-byte diagnostic frames. The environment is classified into three regions: CLEAR (distance $>$ 650\,mm), WARN (450--650\,mm hysteresis band with reduced speed), and BLOCK ($\leq$450\,mm, avoidance triggered). The 200\,mm hysteresis gap prevents state thrashing near the threshold boundary. A 3-frame debounce counter further suppresses false positives from radar glint, and a 300\,ms frame timeout resets stale obstacle data.

\textit{Avoidance strategy and inherent limitations.} Upon BLOCK detection, the vehicle executes a timed open-loop maneuver: stop-confirm (100\,ms), stop-stabilize (120\,ms), turn-out (360\,ms at speed 240, PWM duty cycle, 0--500 range), arc bypass (900\,ms with differential inner/outer speeds of 160/320), turn-in (360\,ms at speed 240), and line reacquisition (1600\,ms timeout). The avoidance direction uses a heuristic fallback chain: configured preferred side, then last known line direction, then reverse retry (maximum 2 attempts). An emergency stop triggers if distance falls below 280\,mm during the maneuver. Fig.~\ref{fig:avoidance} illustrates the trajectory.

The timed sequence provides functional obstacle circumvention for static obstacles under consistent conditions, but remains inherently sensitive to variations in battery voltage, motor wear, and floor friction. This sensitivity is a sensor configuration limitation rather than a tuning deficiency.

\subsection{Wireless Communication Subsystem}

\textit{Integration challenge.} Checkpoint reports follow the format $\text{TEAM=15,NAME=TDPS,CP=}n\text{,TIME=MM:SS}$, transmitted via the EWM22A LoRa module at 9600\,baud with race timing initiated at calibration completion. The primary challenge in integrating the wireless subsystem with the line-following state machine is that LoRa AT command sequences can block the CPU for 50--200\,ms per transmission, far exceeding the 10\,ms control-loop period. A direct function-call coupling between the state machine and the wireless driver would therefore compromise real-time control guarantees.

\textit{Solution architecture.} The integration employs a weak-symbol hook mechanism. The core state machine defines externally visible hook functions annotated with GCC weak-symbol attributes, providing default empty implementations that compile to no-ops. The wireless module overrides these symbols at link time, receiving checkpoint events without the line-following code having any compile-time dependency on the wireless subsystem. This link-time dependency injection enables the two subsystems to be developed, tested, and verified independently, then linked with zero modifications to the core control logic. All LoRa communication is driven by a per-invocation tick function that processes a single step and returns immediately, ensuring that the wireless subsystem never blocks the 10\,ms control loop. Checkpoint messages are enqueued into a fixed-size ring buffer (depth 16) and dequeued asynchronously with a minimum 500\,ms inter-transmission interval. Fig.~\ref{fig:lora_seq} illustrates the non-blocking communication pipeline.

\textit{Fault tolerance.} The transmission pipeline incorporates three-tier fault tolerance: per-message timeout (800\,ms) for AT command response, optional acknowledgment timeout (300\,ms) for delivery confirmation, and automatic retry (maximum 2 attempts) for failed transmissions.

\begin{figure}[!t]

\section{Results}

\subsection{Simulator Parameter Sweep Results}

The coarse sweep established the viability of the PD + curvature-feedforward architecture by mapping the parameter landscape across 36 configurations. The persistent superiority of $v_{\text{base}}=280$ over 400 indicates that, at the higher speed, the sensor preview geometry no longer provides sufficient correction lead time, producing oscillation amplitude large enough to degrade detection reliability. Similarly, $C_{\text{max}}=300$ outperforming 180 reflects the need for sufficient steering authority on tight geometry (90$^\circ$ and 180$^\circ$ turns), where a constrained command ceiling causes persistent understeer. The dominance of $k_d \in [1.0, 1.2]$ over $[0.4, 0.8]$ reveals that damping is the binding constraint in this system: under-damped configurations accumulate cross-track error through successive curves, whereas well-damped settings recover promptly. Table~\ref{tab:sweep-results} presents representative results from both sweeps, and Fig.~\ref{fig:sweep} visualizes the coarse sweep heatmap and kff ablation analysis side by side.

The fine sweep isolates the contribution of curvature feedforward (Eq.~\ref{eq:correction}) by fixing $(k_p=0.25, k_d=1.20)$ and varying $k_{\text{ff}}$. The optimal $k_{\text{ff}}=0.0008$ (Table~\ref{tab:sweep-results}) reduces correction variance by 19\%, shifting the control burden from the reactive proportional term to an anticipatory feedforward channel that anticipates curvature from the rate of change of lateral error. This reduction in corrective variance directly translates to smoother motor duty cycles and lower mechanical stress. The abrupt performance degradation at $k_{\text{ff}} \geq 0.0012$, manifesting as straight-line micro-oscillation, reveals a fundamental trade-off: excessive feedforward gain amplifies sensor quantization noise in straight segments where $de/dt$ is small and dominated by discretization artifacts. Table~\ref{tab:sweep-results} (lower panel) and Fig.~\ref{fig:kff_ablation} quantify this transition.

The 16 scenarios span six categories that collectively exercise the major failure modes of an autonomous line follower: track geometry (circle, figure-8, patio), initial offset (40\,mm left/right), sensor fault injection (Gaussian noise $\sigma=0.028$, 1\% dropout, 6\% contrast reduction), motor non-idealities (7\% left/right mismatch, first-order lag $\tau=0.10$\,s), full-course realism (patio-real 22.40\,m map with checkpoint/finish/boundary), and radar perturbation (fixed and random obstacles). The score never falls below 95 across all six categories, demonstrating consistent performance across all fault categories. The sole systematic weakness is the lateral offset category, where mean absolute error increases from approximately 2\,mm (nominal) to 15--19\,mm. This degradation is attributable to the 22\,cm sensor preview geometry: when the vehicle begins displaced from the line, the lookahead sensors register the error only after the vehicle has committed significant lateral velocity, imposing a minimum correction time that the PD controller cannot reduce below the sensor--actuator geometric delay.

The full-course simulation (90\,s, wireless enabled, 2 checkpoints, 22.40\,m track) achieved an overall score of 92.81 with 99.6\% line detection rate and 0.300\,s maximum lost time. The nominal and left-offset scenarios exceeded 94 points; the right-offset scenario revealed a 0.30\,s line-loss event consistent with the asymmetric left-side boundary corridor. Obstacle recovery succeeded in all scenarios; however, progress reached only 71--74\% of the course within the 90\,s time limit, indicating that $v_{\text{base}}=280$ is below the speed needed for full-course completion within competition time. This is a deliberate design choice: speed was traded for reliability after the coarse sweep demonstrated that $v_{\text{base}}=400$ degraded performance across all scenarios through increased oscillation amplitude.

\subsection{On-Car Validation}

\subsubsection{Nominal Performance}

On-car testing followed a two-phase protocol. The debug profile ($v_{\text{base}}=200$, $k_p=0.15$, $k_d=0.60$) was used for hardware shakedown, after which the competition track profile ($v_{\text{base}}=280$, $k_p=0.25$, $k_d=1.20$, $k_{\text{ff}}=0.0008$) was applied for full-performance validation. The track profile achieved stable line tracking on straight segments with no visible oscillation, maintained line contact through 90$^\circ$ and 180$^\circ$ turns at $v_{\text{min}}=60$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:speed}), and successfully navigated U-turns where the lead compensation sequence triggered at the correct geometric advance.

\subsubsection{Avoidance Validation}

The avoidance maneuver sequence was tested against a stationary 60$\times$80\,mm obstacle placed on the track centerline. The vehicle successfully executed all four phases (confirm, turn-out 360\,ms, bypass 900\,ms, turn-in 360\,ms, reacquire $\leq$1600\,ms) and reacquired the line on both left-side and right-side bypass attempts. However, the effective turn radius varied by approximately 15\% between full battery charge (8.4\,V) and partial discharge (7.4\,V). This discrepancy is directly attributable to the open-loop timing model, which commands fixed motor durations without compensating for the voltage-dependent speed reduction. The 280\,mm emergency stop distance was never triggered during controlled tests, confirming adequate safety margin.

\subsubsection{Sim-to-Physical Discrepancies}

Two systematic discrepancies between simulation and physical testing were identified. First, the simulator predicted zero line-loss events across all 16 quick scenarios, whereas on-car tests experienced occasional loss at the figure-8 track crossover point due to spatially correlated glare from the map lamination --- a correlated disturbance not captured by the simulator's independent per-channel Gaussian noise model. Second, the lead compensation advance (18 ticks at reduced speed 65) required empirical recalibration after each hardware change (sensor remount, chassis weight redistribution), confirming that time-based geometric compensation without encoder feedback is sensitive to mechanical parameter drift.

LoRa field tests over 10--15\,m open-air range confirmed correct checkpoint message formatting and zero main-loop blocking incidents attributable to wireless activity, validating the weak-symbol hook architecture described in Section~III.C.

\section{Analysis and Discussion}

\subsection{Why Simplification Works: Parameter Decoupling and Failure Mode Analysis}

The 62-to-6 parameter reduction improved both absolute performance and tunability more than any incremental PID gain tuning.

The kff ablation provides the strongest quantitative evidence: $k_{\text{ff}}=0.0008$ reduces correction standard deviation by 19\% not by adding steering authority, but by shifting correction burden from the reactive proportional term to the anticipatory feedforward term, leaving more headroom under the $C_{\text{max}}=300$ clamp. The coarse sweep's $(k_p, k_d)$ heatmap exhibited a smooth, single-peak surface rather than a rugged multi-peak landscape, suggesting negligible coupling among the remaining six parameters. This smooth landscape is consistent with a single optimum in the tested range.

The simplify-first approach applies to any resource-constrained embedded control system with multiple interacting mechanisms. In resource-constrained embedded systems, where tuning effort is expensive and computational margins are narrow, reducing the number of interacting mechanisms is a higher-leverage intervention than optimizing the gains of individual controllers~\cite{skogestad2003simple}. A smaller parameter space with fewer couplings is not only easier to search but also more robust to environmental variation, because fewer mechanisms can enter unexpected resonance under untested operating conditions.

\subsection{Simulator Fidelity: Model Limitations and Ordinal Validity}

The simulator's core value lies in relative comparison --- determining which parameter combination performs better --- rather than in absolute score prediction. The following limitations do not invalidate the sweep conclusions, which depend on parameter ranking rather than absolute scores. A simulator does not need to be a high-fidelity digital twin to be useful; it needs to preserve the partial ordering of the objective function over the parameter space, a weaker requirement that makes simulation-based tuning practical for resource-constrained teams.

Three categories of discrepancy were identified between simulated and physical operation. First, time-based lead compensation using tick counts produces different travel distances at full battery (8.4\,V) versus nominal (7.4\,V), with an estimated 10--15\% variation. Second, the sensor noise model's independent per-channel Gaussian injection does not capture spatially correlated artifacts (floor dust, map creases, reflective glare) that trigger noise rejection more frequently on physical tracks. Third, open-loop avoidance timing requires empirical scaling factors of 1.1--1.3$\times$ on the physical car due to unmodeled static friction, Coulomb friction, and voltage-dependent torque constant.

Despite these limitations, the simulator enabled 30-second feedback loops versus an estimated 15--20 minutes per on-car trial, yielding a 30--40$\times$ iteration speedup. The 63-group sweep's ordinal conclusions --- that $k_{\text{ff}}=0.0008$ outperforms pure PD, that 6 parameters suffice for 97+\% scores, and that the optimal $(k_p, k_d)$ region is robust to noise --- all held when validated on the physical car, confirming that ordinal fidelity is sufficient for parameter optimization in this class of system.

\subsection{Cross-Subsystem Coupling Under the 10\,ms Constraint}

The 10\,ms control loop period is the binding constraint across all three subsystems. The line-following controller consumes approximately 0.3\,ms per iteration (sensor read + PD+kff computation + motor command), leaving approximately 9.7\,ms of headroom per cycle. Radar parsing occupies an additional 0.2--0.5\,ms when UART bytes are available, and LoRa AT command processing adds 0.1--0.3\,ms per tick. The non-blocking tick architecture ensures that radar and LoRa processing can never accumulate more than one discrete step per control cycle, bounding worst-case jitter to under 1.0\,ms --- well within the 10\,ms budget.

The most consequential interaction occurs when radar BLOCK triggers an avoidance sequence. During avoidance, the line-following state machine is suspended (RUNNING $\rightarrow$ AVOID\_PREP), and the 10\,ms loop continues executing the timed open-loop maneuver. Because LoRa transmission is advanced by the same tick, checkpoint messages can still be sent during avoidance --- a design property confirmed by the full-course simulation where wireless transmission succeeded in all scenarios despite concurrent obstacle events. However, line reacquisition after avoidance imposes a transient recovery cost: the vehicle must transition through AVOID\_REACQUIRE $\rightarrow$ RUNNING $\rightarrow$ re-stabilize the control loop, during which $\dot{e}$ is undefined and the feedforward term contributes nothing for approximately 200\,ms.

Three system-level bottlenecks constrain further improvement. The forward-only radar perception is the most critical single-point failure: the heuristic direction selection will choose incorrectly when the obstacle is opposite the approach direction on a curved segment --- a sensor geometry limitation, not a parameter issue. Open-loop avoidance timing creates nine tuned parameters (eight timing values plus one safety distance) requiring recalibration after any hardware change and cannot adapt to varying obstacle widths. The 22\,cm sensor preview, while enabling the derivative-feedforward combination, requires geometric lead compensation that is currently time-approximate rather than position-accurate. \section{Conclusions and Future Work}

This work demonstrates that a 6-parameter simplified PD plus curvature feedforward controller, systematically optimized through 63-group simulator-based parameter sweeping, achieves a regression score of 97.65/100 with 100\% line detection and zero off-line time across 16 heterogeneous fault-injection scenarios. The controller was derived from root-cause failure analysis of a legacy 62-parameter architecture: seven competing mechanisms were categorized into threshold-based, gain-competing, and rate-limiting classes, and eliminated with documented evidence that each removal either improved or preserved performance. The curvature feedforward term ($k_{\text{ff}}=0.0008$) reduced steering correction standard deviation by 19\% versus pure PD, confirming that the 22\,cm sensor preview geometry can be exploited as a preview advantage rather than a control burden. These findings extend beyond this specific platform to other resource-constrained embedded control systems.

Three engineering findings extend beyond this specific platform. First, the simplify-first methodology reverses the conventional tuning workflow: competing mechanisms are eliminated before the remaining parameters are optimized, producing a smooth single-peak parameter landscape whose global optimum is discoverable through coarse-to-fine sweeping. Second, the 30--40$\times$ iteration speedup of the offline simulator depended not on high-fidelity absolute prediction but on preserving the partial ordering of the objective function --- a weaker requirement that makes simulation-based tuning practical for resource-constrained teams. Third, the weak-symbol hook mechanism, a standard GCC pattern, achieved formal subsystem decoupling at zero runtime overhead: the line-following state machine and wireless module were developed, tested, and linked independently, then integrated without changes to the core logic.

The primary limitations are hardware-constrained: forward-only radar sensing makes avoidance direction inherently heuristic, the nine-parameter timed open-loop avoidance sequence is sensitive to battery voltage and motor wear, and time-based lead compensation introduces position error that varies with speed. Future priority directions are lateral sensor addition for perception-driven avoidance, encoder-closed-loop lead compensation, and extension of the simulator noise model to include spatially correlated artifacts characteristic of physical track surfaces.
